The Characters of Noli Me Tangere

Image of the Character Name of the Character Description of the Character
Crisostomo Ibarra Also known as Juan Crisostomo Ibarra y Magsalin, he is the main protagonist of Jose Rizal's story, Noli Me Tangere. Ibarra is a young, Spanish mestizo who studied for seven years in Europe. He is regarded as someone sophisticated and with high intelligence, making the priests intimidated by him. Following the death of his father, he returned to his hometown and wanted to establish a proper school there, only to face numerous obstacles from the local figures.
Maria Clara Also known as Maria Clara de los Santos, she is the mestiza heroine of the story. Maria Clara is raised as the daughter of Kapitan Tiyago, and is betrothed to Crisostomo Ibarra. Her name and character has since become a tradional, feminine ideal in Filipino culture.
Tenyente Guevarra A morally upright man of Spanish descent, he is the lieutenant of the Civil Guard. He is one of the few people who openly support the Ibarras and is vocal about his dislike of Padre Damaso's control. He informs Crisostomo Ibarra about his father and how Padre Damaso was involved in his death.
Padre Damaso Padre Damaso is known as an arrogant and pedantic Spanish friar living in the Philippines. Despite having lived amongst Filipinos and hearing their confessions for over twenty years, he is barely able to speak or understand Tagalog. A shameless loudmouth, he slanders nonreligious citizens who he thinks undermines his power.
Kapitan Tiago Also known as Don Santiago de los Santos, he is a Filipino socialite and well-respected member of the country's wealthy elite. Close with high-ranking clergy members such as Padre Salvi and Padre Damaso, Kapitan Tiyago is one of the richest property owners in Manila and San Diego. He is concerned with keeping up the appearance of respectability, which is why he wants his daughter, Maria Clara, to marry an affluent man with ample social capital.
Don Rafael Ibarra He is the father of Crisostomo Ibarra and who died before the novel's opening pages. A critic of the corrupt practices of the Spanish friars, he earns the ire of the vitriolic Padre Damaso, who accuses him of sedition and heresy. He passed away in prison before his name can be cleared.
